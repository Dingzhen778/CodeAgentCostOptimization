# Writing Rules

## Rule 1: Separate framing from evidence

Before methods and experiments, write:

- why the problem matters
- what the task setting is
- what the literature has covered
- what gap remains
- what this thesis intends to study

Do not write:

- experimental numbers
- observed best method
- current resolved counts
- final conclusions from unfinished experiments

## Rule 2: Treat current observations as motivation unless validated in experiment chapters

Examples of acceptable phrasing:

- repository-level repair may introduce substantial context overhead
- multi-turn agent interaction can accumulate redundant historical observations
- cost optimization may require focusing on code understanding rather than only final prompt length

Examples of phrasing to avoid before experiment chapters:

- the read stage is the dominant cost source
- method A outperforms method B
- the hybrid method is currently best

## Rule 3: Prefer local drafting before formal write-back

When structure is still changing:

- draft in `SYSU_documents/`
- confirm outline or section logic
- then sync into `overleaf/paper/data/*.tex`

## Rule 4: Replace stale template content structurally

If a thesis chapter still reflects the old Web3 topic:

- replace chapter title and section skeleton first
- then write new content
- avoid mixing new code-agent prose into old Web3 structure

## Rule 5: Use citations conservatively without search

If precise recent references are needed and local materials are insufficient:

- stop short of inventing citations
- provide a search plan
- mark which claims need literature support
